Key Findings & Recommendations

Observed Patterns

  • Strong acute signal: Mean HR rise of +22 bpm within 30-90 min confirms T3 creates detectable physiological response
  • Time-of-day effect dominates: Evening doses show higher baseline HR (~75-80 bpm) vs morning (~55-60 bpm), which affects response interpretation
  • High variability: HR rise ranges from -25 to +102 bpm - individual responses vary dramatically day-to-day
  • Confounders minimal: Only 9/247 events (3.6%) have Vyvanse/methylphenidate active - most data is clean
  • 0 mcg evening events: These show HR rise (~15-20 bpm) despite no T3 at that dose - likely residual T3 from earlier + circadian effects

Recommendations for Analysis

  • Focus on morning doses: Lower baseline HR and fewer confounding factors make morning doses cleanest for analysis
  • Exclude 0 mcg from dose-response: These are confounded by earlier T3 doses, not true controls
  • Use scatter plots over bar charts: High variability means averages hide important individual patterns
  • Consider baseline-adjusted analysis: Normalize by baseline HR or calculate % change instead of absolute bpm

Critical Analysis Update (Jan 29, 2026)

Acute HR Response is NOT Predictive of Feeling State

After comprehensive analysis including menstrual cycle phases, recovery scores, and sleep data, we found:

All dose levels (5-10 mcg/day) show similar HR response magnitude (~22 bpm)
The "overactivated, less clear" period (7.5 mcg) = 22.4 bpm
The "happy, great dose A+" period (10 mcg) = 22.0 bpm

Recovery/sleep correlations are near zero:
• Recovery score vs HR rise: r = -0.04
• Sleep duration vs HR rise: r = -0.05

Menstrual phase shows no significant effect:
All phases (menstrual, follicular, ovulatory, luteal) = 21-22 bpm average

Critical Data Gap: STRUGGLING Period

The period when Ariel felt worst ("cold, fragile, dull, crying") from Oct 18-28, 2025 has 0% HR data coverage. We cannot compare struggling period signatures to optimal periods.

Next Steps for Better Predictive Modeling

  • Multi-day HRV trends: Look at 3-7 day patterns instead of acute post-dose response
  • Daily symptom tracking: Need actual journal entries (energy, brain fog) tied to specific dates
  • Recovery score trends: Rolling 3-day averages may be more informative
  • Blood work correlation: Map lab values (TSH, Free T4, Total T3) to wearable patterns
