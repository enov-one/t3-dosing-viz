Can consumer wearable data objectively detect whether a thyroid hormone dose is working — potentially before the patient even knows?

Patient: Ariel Condition: Post-thyroidectomy (BRAF V600E+ PTC) Genotype: DIO2 rs225014 CC (~30% reduced T4→T3 conversion) Data: Whoop wearable, Oct 2024 – Jan 2026 Protocols tested: 7

The Question

Ariel has no thyroid (post-thyroidectomy for papillary thyroid cancer). She takes exogenous thyroid hormone daily. Her DIO2 CC genotype means she converts T4→T3 ~30% less efficiently than average, justifying combination T3+T4 therapy rather than T4-only.

Over 15 months, she's tried 7 different dosing protocols, rating each from her subjective experience. The question: is there an objective wearable signal that can predict which dose is right, faster than waiting weeks for subjective clarity?

This journal tracks the chronological evolution of hypotheses and experiments as we search for that signal.

Experiments (Chronological)

Exp. 1
Acute HR Response to T3 Dose
Jan 29, 2026

Hypothesis: T3 causes a measurable acute heart rate rise within 30-90 minutes of ingestion. Can the magnitude of this rise differentiate between good and bad protocols?

Method: Matched 247 dose events (from Medisafe) with continuous Whoop HR data. Computed the HR rise from pre-dose baseline to 30-90 minute post-dose window for each event.

Partial — Signal exists but doesn't differentiate protocols

T3 causes a reliable +22 bpm acute HR rise (clinically significant). However, the magnitude is similar across all protocols — it reflects T3 potency, not dose appropriateness. A 2.5mcg dose causes +20 bpm whether the overall protocol is rated A+ or C.

Conclusion: T3 acute effect is real and measurable, but it's a pharmacokinetic signal, not a dose-optimization signal. Moving on.

Exp. 2
NightSignal: Overnight RHR Deviation Detection
Jan 29, 2026

Hypothesis: Adapting the Snyder Lab's COVID detection methodology (Alavi et al., Nature Medicine 2022), can we detect thyroid dose deviations from a 28-day rolling baseline of overnight resting heart rate?

Method: Compute overnight RHR (00:00–07:00) daily, establish a 28-day rolling median baseline, flag deviations using a Green→Yellow→Red state machine. Bidirectional: elevated RHR = possible overdosing, depressed = underdosing.

Partial — Detects extremes but misses subtlety

P6 (overdosed, B-) shows +2.8 bpm deviation and only 30% green days. P7 (optimal, A+) shows −0.1 bpm and 59% green days. But the method can't distinguish between A+ and B+ protocols — both look "green." Also, the F-rated STRUGGLING period (P4) has zero overnight data.

Conclusion: Good at catching gross overdosing (P6). Not sensitive enough for fine-grained dose optimization. The 28-day baseline window is too long for quick detection.

Exp. 3
Multi-Signal Protocol Report Card
Feb 17, 2026

Hypothesis: By comparing 13 different signals across all 6 analyzable protocols (RHR, HRV, skin temp, recovery, deep sleep, REM, sleep hours, efficiency, strain, workouts, anxiety rate, depression rate), can we identify which signals discriminate between good and bad protocols?

Method: Compute per-protocol means, run Kruskal-Wallis non-parametric tests (with chi-square CDF computed from scratch), compute pairwise Cohen's d effect sizes, generate radar chart comparison.

Confirmed — Observational differences, but not predictive

8 of 12 signals significantly different across protocols (p<0.05). T4-only (P1) is objectively worse: RHR 63 vs 58-60 bpm, HRV 27 vs 30-32 ms, Recovery 49 vs 53-63. Skin temperature tracks T4-equivalent dose. But these are retrospective observations, not predictions. You can only compute these after weeks of data collection.

Conclusion: Confirmed that T3 therapy creates objectively measurable improvements over T4-only. Useful for validation, but the goal is something predictive that works within days.

Exp. 4
Dose Adequacy Score (DAS) — Directional Prediction
Feb 17, 2026

Hypothesis: Combine 5 key wearable signals (skin temp, RHR, HRV, recovery, deep sleep) into a single daily composite z-score (the "DAS"). If DAS improves after a dose change, the new dose is better. Test at 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 10, 14-day windows.

Method: Z-score each signal against the patient's own baseline, direction-adjust (invert RHR since lower=better), average into a single daily number. Compare pre-transition vs post-transition DAS at each time window. If DAS goes up and the dose actually got better (or vice versa), count as correct prediction.

Failed — 0% accuracy at 7-day and 14-day windows

Simple directional DAS prediction gets 0% accuracy at the most clinically relevant windows (7 and 14 days). Partial accuracy (~67%) at 2-day window, but this is unstable and not clinically useful.

Why it failed: Wearable metrics track physiological ACTIVATION, not dose OPTIMALITY. The overdosed protocol (P6) shows "good" individual metrics (lower RHR, warmer skin, higher HRV) because the body is hyperactivated. But the patient feels terrible — jittery, overactivated, less clear. This is the inverted-U problem: too little = bad metrics, too much = good metrics but bad feeling. Direction can't solve this.

Exp. 5
DAS Target-Zone Model
Feb 17, 2026

Hypothesis: Instead of "DAS goes up = good," try: "DAS near zero = optimal." A+ protocols should cluster at DAS ≈ 0, underdosed at DAS < -0.15, overdosed at DAS > +0.15.

Failed — DAS doesn't separate A+ from non-A+

Per-protocol mean DAS values don't cluster as predicted. The z-score normalization baseline was contaminated by 19 months of pre-protocol data (Mar 2023 onward), washing out protocol-specific differences. Even with protocol-period-only normalization, the mean DAS values overlap: P2(A+) = 0.001, P5(B+) = 0.050, P6(B-) = 0.155. The separation isn't clean enough.

Conclusion: The problem isn't the normalization — it's that the level of DAS can't distinguish optimal from near-optimal. Need a different property of the signal.

Exp. 6
DAS Stability (Churn) — The Homeostasis Signal
Feb 18, 2026

Hypothesis: Instead of asking "where is DAS?", ask "how much does DAS bounce around day-to-day?" When the dose is right, the body achieves homeostasis and wearable signals stabilize. Measure the mean absolute day-to-day change in DAS ("churn"). Lower churn = more stable = better dose.

Method: For each protocol period, compute DAS daily churn as the mean of |DAS(day) − DAS(day−1)| across consecutive days. Correlate with patient ratings using Spearman rank correlation. Test a threshold rule (churn < 0.50) for binary classification.

Success — Perfect top-3/bottom-3 separation (p = 0.02)

Spearman r = −0.76, p = 0.02. All 3 A+ protocols (P2, P7, P3) rank as the 3 most stable. All 3 non-optimal protocols (P6, P1, P5) rank as the 3 least stable. This is a perfect rank-order separation of good vs. bad protocols.

P2 (A+): churn = 0.431
P7 (A+): churn = 0.443
P3 (A+): churn = 0.498
P6 (B-): churn = 0.596
P1 (C): churn = 0.612
P5 (B+): churn = 0.664

Why this works when direction failed: The inverted-U problem meant overdosed protocols had "good" individual metrics. But they were unstable. The body can't find equilibrium when overdosed — one day HR is too low, next day it rebounds. Skin temp swings. Recovery oscillates. Stability captures this instability that the direction metric misses completely.

Transition detection: At dose transitions, churn increases within 5-7 days when the new dose is wrong. P5→P6 (good→overdosed): churn jumped from 0.426 to 0.655 within 7 days, correctly flagging the dose as problematic before the patient fully recognized it.

Exp. 7
Exploratory Predictive Analysis — Hypothesis Testing
Feb 19, 2026

Purpose: Before building predictive algorithms, test 5 core hypotheses about wearable signal patterns to determine which approaches are viable.

4 of 5 hypotheses confirmed

Q1 (Concordance): ✓ A+ protocols have distinct inter-signal correlation fingerprint (Frobenius distance = 0.854).
Q2 (Discrimination): ✓ Individual signals can separate A+ from non-A+ days.
Q3 (Circadian): ✗ Within-day HR patterns show weak protocol differences.
Q4 (Phase Confound): ✓ Menstrual phase causes minimal confounding across protocols.
Q5 (Transitions): ✓ Change-points detectable at dose transitions.

Key finding: The Allostatic Concordance Index (ACI) — a composite metric measuring how well wearable signals "agree" with each other — successfully validates as a protocol quality indicator. A+ protocols cluster together (mean distance from A+ template = 0.269), while non-A+ protocols are farther away (mean distance = 0.959).

Generalizability: Q2 (signal discrimination) and Q4 (menstrual confounding) are generalizable to anyone tracking "good days" vs. "bad days" with Whoop. No thyroid protocols required.

Next Steps: Generalizable Algorithms

Most of the dashboards above are specific to Ariel's thyroid dosing experiments. However, two algorithms are generalizable and can work for anyone with a Whoop wearable — no thyroid condition or medication experiments required.

✅ Recovery Intelligence Engine

What it is: A 3-module predictive engine that learns your personal physiology and helps you optimize daily behaviors.

What it does:

  • Menstrual Cycle Baselines: If you menstruate, it learns your normal 14-point recovery swing across your cycle using harmonic regression. This prevents you from panicking when recovery dips mid-follicular phase — it's just your cycle, not a problem.
  • Next-Day Recovery Predictor: Predicts tomorrow's recovery score tonight with 72% direction accuracy. Uses 43 features (sleep architecture, strain, behaviors, cycle phase, rolling averages). Tells you which factors are pulling recovery up or down.
  • Behavior Impact Scorecard: Runs proper statistics (Mann-Whitney U, confounder-adjusted regression, FDR correction) to tell you which of your tracked behaviors actually work. For Ariel: meditation showed +7.4 recovery points (p=0.009, FDR-significant). Caffeine showed -21.7 points but with high variance.

Who it's for: Anyone with Whoop who wants to run N=1 experiments ("Does magnesium actually help my recovery?") or predict bad days before they happen.

✅ Exploratory Predictive Analysis (Partial)

What it is: Tests 5 hypotheses about wearable signal patterns. Two are generalizable without medication experiments.

What it does:

  • Q4: Menstrual Phase Confounding: Quantifies how much your cycle affects recovery, HRV, and RHR. Prevents misattributing cycle effects to other variables. Works for anyone who menstruates.
  • Q2: Signal Discrimination: Tests which individual Whoop signals (RHR, HRV, sleep efficiency, etc.) best separate "good days" from "bad days" using your subjective ratings. Tells you which metric to pay attention to.

Who it's for: Anyone tracking how they feel day-to-day who wants to know which wearable signals correlate with their subjective state. Requires journaling "good/bad day" ratings.

🔬 Validation Strategy for New Data

To confirm these algorithms give correct answers when applied to Ariel's newer data (or anyone else's data), use temporal validation. Two complementary approaches:

1

Temporal Hold-Out Testing

What: Split data chronologically. Train on old data, test on new data you haven't shown the model.
Example: Train on all data through Jan 15, 2026 → Predict on Jan 16 - Feb 28, 2026.
Check: MAE still ~18? Direction accuracy still ~72%? Meditation still +7.4 pts? Cycle amplitude still ~14 pts?

Pro: Standard ML validation
Con: One-time test
2

Prospective Prediction Log

What: Real-world validation. Predict each evening, compare to actual next morning.
Process: Evening of Day N → Model predicts tomorrow's recovery → Morning of Day N+1 → Whoop reports actual → Log: (date, predicted, actual, error, direction_correct).
After 30 days: Calculate rolling MAE, direction accuracy, calibration curves.

Pro: Catches model drift over time
Con: Takes 30+ days
+

Optional: Automated Validation Script

Not a separate strategy — just automation of Steps 1 & 2 above. Build a script that runs both validation approaches and generates an HTML report showing out-of-sample MAE, behavior effect replication, cycle pattern consistency, and feature importance stability. Saves manual work when you have new data.
